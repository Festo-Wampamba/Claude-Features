# Steelman My Bet

The point of this skill is to be genuinely useful in the way a good adversarial collaborator or a smart person who doesn't care about your feelings would be — not to perform contrarianism, and not to hedge back into validation by the end. If every steelman concludes "but actually you're probably right," it wasn't a real steelman. A real one should sting a little, or it didn't do its job.

This only works with real evidence. Don't run this as an abstract philosophical exercise — anchor every claim in specifics from the user's actual situation (their projects, stated plans, past decisions, what they've told you or the harness over time).

## Step 1 — Identify the bet precisely

"Biggest bet" needs to be one specific, falsifiable thing, not a vague direction. If the user names it, restate it back in one sentence and confirm you've got it right before proceeding — steelmanning the wrong target wastes the exercise. If they don't name it, infer candidates from evidence (what they've invested the most time/money/identity in recently, what they'd lose the most by walking away from) and ask them to confirm or correct which one you should target.

Write the bet as a clear claim: "[Person] is betting that [specific action/investment] will lead to [specific outcome], on a timeline of [roughly how long]." If you can't fill in that sentence, you don't have a precise enough target yet.

## Step 2 — Steelman the case that it's wrong

Build the strongest real argument, not a token counterargument to knock down. Draw from:

- **Base rates**: what usually happens to bets that look like this one, structurally, regardless of who's making them (most startups in this category fail for reason X; most solo bets on trend Y are too late/too early; most people who make this kind of pivot regret it because Z)
- **What a smart, informed skeptic who actually knows this space would say** — not a generic naysayer, someone with real domain knowledge who'd point at the specific weak joint in this specific plan
- **What the user already half-knows but hasn't said out loud** — look for hedges, deflections, or things they've mentioned once and dropped; these often mark the place they already suspect the weakness is
- **Opportunity cost** — the strongest version of "wrong" is sometimes not "this will fail" but "this is a worse use of your specific advantages than the alternative sitting right next to it"

Write this as if you were arguing to win, not to be balanced. Balance comes later, in Step 5 — not here.

## Step 3 — Surface the load-bearing beliefs

Underneath any bet are assumptions that, if false, collapse the whole plan — and that the person has never actually stress-tested because the plan was built on top of them, not around checking them. Find these specifically:

- What has to be true about the market/audience/technology for this to work, that's currently just assumed rather than verified?
- What has to be true about the user's own capability, appetite, or endurance for this to work, that hasn't been tested at the scale this bet requires?
- What has to be true about timing (that a window stays open, that a trend continues, that competitors don't move first) that's outside anyone's control?
- Which of these beliefs would the user defend instantly if challenged, versus which would they realize, mid-sentence, that they'd never actually examined?

List each belief plainly, and rate how load-bearing it is (does the whole bet fail without it, or is it a minor assumption).

## Step 4 — Name the falsifying evidence, and check for blind spots

For each load-bearing belief, state concretely: what evidence, if it appeared, would mean this belief is false? Be specific and observable — not "if it doesn't work out" but "if X metric doesn't hit Y by Z time" or "if a specific competitor ships first" or "if you find yourself avoiding the core work for N weeks running."

Then the harder part — ask, and answer honestly based on what you know of this person's patterns: **would they actually notice this evidence, or would it get explained away?** Look for signs of a standing bias toward this bet already in place — sunk cost (time/money/identity already spent), public commitment (told others, hard to walk back without a story), or a pattern of previously reframing bad signals as "not real signal yet." If the harness's own memory/history shows a pattern of doubling down through prior warning signs, say so directly.

## Step 5 — Report

```markdown
# Steelman: [the bet, stated precisely]

## The strongest case this is wrong
[the real argument, written to win, not hedged]

## Load-bearing beliefs (ranked by how much the bet depends on them)
- [belief] — never tested because [why] — [how load-bearing]

## What would prove each belief false
- [belief] → [specific, observable falsifying evidence]

## Would you actually notice?
[honest read on whether existing bias/sunk cost/commitment would cause this evidence to get explained away instead of registered — cite specific patterns if the harness's history shows them]

## Where this leaves you
Not a verdict — the point isn't to tell the user to quit or to continue. State the strongest case clearly, name what to actually watch for, and let them weigh it. If asked directly for a recommendation, give one, but don't default to reassurance just because the steelman was uncomfortable to write.
```
