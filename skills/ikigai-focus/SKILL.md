# Ikigai Focus

Ikigai is usually drawn as four overlapping circles: what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, what you can be paid for. The failure mode of doing this analysis badly is picking one circle (usually "what's hot in AI right now") and calling it the answer. The job here is to actually find the overlap using real evidence from this specific person, not to hand them a generic trend report.

This is a genuinely personal, high-stakes recommendation — get it wrong and it wastes months of someone's life. Take the interview seriously, don't rush to an answer, and be explicit about your confidence level in the final recommendation.

## Step 1 — Gather real evidence before forming any opinion

Don't theorize from a couple of data points. Pull from every available source:

- **This harness's history**: recent projects, memory/observations, session activity — what has the person actually spent time building, debugging, and caring enough about to configure tooling around? Time and tooling investment is a stronger signal than stated interest.
- **Public writing and projects**: anything the user points you to (blog, GitHub, portfolio, social presence) — read it for real, don't skim for keywords. Look for what they write about unprompted vs. what's clearly obligation/client work.
- **Web search** on the user (if they're comfortable with this — confirm before searching for someone by name) for public presence, to see how the outside world currently perceives their work versus how they'd describe it themselves — that gap is itself useful data.
- **Field context**: what's actually happening in AI, their specific technical niche, and adjacent industries right now — not a generic "AI is big" observation, but specifically what's changing that intersects with what this person already knows how to do.

## Step 2 — Interview for the parts evidence can't reach

Files and history show what happened, not why, or what's missing. Ask directly — don't guess at answers you could just ask for:

- Of everything you're currently doing, which parts energize you and which do you do out of inertia or obligation?
- Is there a version of this where you're the builder, the collaborator, or the person a company hires — which of those feels right, and does that answer change by project?
- What's the actual money situation — do you need this to be lucrative soon, or do you have runway to bet on fulfillment first and let lucrative catch up?
- When people ask what you do, what do you currently say, and does saying it feel true or like a dodge?
- Is there a project on the list you'd protect even if it made no money?

## Step 3 — Find the actual overlap, not the average

Map what you've learned into the four circles specifically for this person (skip the framework theater — don't just draw four circles and call it done):

- **Loves**: evidenced by unprompted time investment, not stated preference alone
- **Good at**: evidenced by depth of work, not breadth of tools touched
- **World needs**: the specific gap in the market/field that this person's actual skill maps to — be concrete about *who* needs this and why now, not "AI is growing"
- **Paid for**: realistic near-term monetization paths given the above — solo, with collaborators, or inside a company — and be honest if the answer is "not yet, here's the bridge"

The overlap is the finding. If the evidence points to genuine tension (e.g. what they love doesn't currently pay, what pays doesn't excite them), say that plainly rather than forcing a false synthesis — the honest answer might be "pursue X for fulfillment, keep Y as the income bridge for now," not a single unified path.

## Step 4 — Recommend, concretely

```markdown
# Ikigai Focus

## What the evidence shows
- Loves: [with evidence]
- Good at: [with evidence]
- World needs: [specific gap, specific audience]
- Paid for: [realistic paths]

## The overlap (the actual recommendation)
[the specific direction — not a category like "AI," the actual thing]

## Confidence and tension
[how strong is this read, and where the four circles don't cleanly agree]

## Concrete next steps
- Possible domain/name, if relevant
- How to fold existing scattered projects into this one narrative (which continue, which pause, which get reframed as evidence of the direction rather than separate things)
- A one-line and a one-paragraph version of "what I do" — the thing they'd actually say at a party or in a work conversation

## What would change this recommendation
[what new evidence, in the next few months, would mean this call was wrong — so it doesn't calcify into an unquestioned identity the same way the drifted-identity problem does elsewhere in the harness]
```

Offer to interview further before finalizing if the evidence gathered in Step 1 feels thin — a rushed answer here is worse than a slower, better one.
